# Executive Brief: Ms. Pac-Man (Business + History)

## One-sentence summary

**Ms. Pac-Man (1982) began as an unauthorized enhancement kit built quickly by a small Massachusetts team, was converted through a Midway deal into an official sequel manufactured in Illinois, sold roughly 117,000-125,000 arcade units as one of the era's highest-grossing arcade products, and later became a textbook example of how fragmented IP rights can limit long-term brand use.**

## Fast timeline (dated)

- **Late May/Early June 1981**: GCC starts the *Crazy Otto* enhancement project.
- **October 29, 1981**: GCC signs the Bally Midway agreement to convert the project into an official sequel.
- **February 3, 1982**: *Ms. Pac-Man* public debut.
- **1982-1988**: Massive arcade rollout; commonly cited unit sales reach **117,000 to 125,000**.
- **2019-2022**: Bandai Namco/AtGames legal conflict highlights ongoing rights complexity around the character and royalties.

## Where it was made

- **Design and core game work**: GCC team around MIT/Cambridge area and Wayland, Massachusetts.
- **Commercial manufacturing and distribution**: Midway operations and factory footprint in the Chicago area (including Franklin Park, Illinois).
- **IP owner context**: Namco in Japan remained central to Pac-Man rights and royalty flow.

## The real business story

The business story is not just "hit sequel." It is:

- an unauthorized derivative becoming an official flagship product,
- a very high-leverage build (small team, short timeline, huge demand),
- and a rights structure that maximized short-term success but created long-run licensing friction.

## Hard-number headline for class

- **Unit sales**: about **117k-125k** cabinets.
- **Likely sell-in scale** (using period machine-price anchors): roughly **$268M-$375M**.
- **Operator coin-drop scale** (using period weekly-earnings anchors): roughly **$1.0B-$1.7B annualized gross** across the installed base during strong periods.

## Bottom-line conclusion

From a business lens, *Ms. Pac-Man* is a rare "tiny build, huge commercial upside" case where legal architecture became almost as important as the product itself.
